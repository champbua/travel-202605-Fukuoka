Coffee at Café Brasileiro

12:30·Lunch
Seiromushi — eel steamed over rice
Yanagawa's signature dish, served the same way for three centuries: eel grilled, glazed, then steamed in a wooden box until the rice beneath turns the colour of dark amber. The kind of meal that explains, without words, why people return to a small town. ※ N4
14:00·On the water
A boat through Yanagawa
A short walk from lunch to the boat station. A boatman will ease your wooden punt under low stone bridges, willows brushing the surface, the only sound his pole turning gently against the canal floor. Seventy minutes you won't want to end — and you'll be on the water as the afternoon light begins to gild the canals. ※ N3

19:30·Dinner
Motsunabe Rakutenchi
Hakata's most beloved dish, in the place that helped invent it. Six cuts of Wagyu offal simmering in a deep miso broth alongside mounds of cabbage and chive, until you can't tell where one ingredient ends and the next begins. Finish with champon noodles dropped straight into what's left. Three hours later you'll feel newly alive.

09:30·Shrine
Dazaifu Tenmangu
Thirty minutes by train to one of Japan's most loved shrines, dedicated to the god of learning. The approach is lined with stalls selling umegae-mochi — small grilled rice cakes filled with plum bean paste, made nowhere else. Buy one warm, eat it as you walk.
📍 Dazaifu Tenmangu ↗
11:00·Museum
Kyushu National Museum
One of only four national museums in Japan, reached from the shrine by a long escalator carved into the hillside. The collection traces a thousand years of cultural exchange between Japan and the rest of Asia — the building itself, all wood and glass and curved roof, is worth the visit alone.
📍 Kyushu National Museum ↗
13:30·Lunch
Light lunch at La Brioche
Back in the city. A French bakery near Ohori Park, the kind of place where bread is still treated like architecture. Buy a baguette, a tart, and a coffee — eat it on a bench under the trees.
📍 La Brioche ↗
15:00·Afternoon
An hour around Ohori Park
Two kilometres of flat path around a central pond, three small islands joined by bridges, and a Japanese garden tucked inside that asks nothing of you. The late-May green is at its softest. Stop on a bench. Don't apologise for being slow.
📍 Ohori Park ↗

The owner walks Yanagibashi Market at dawn and chooses each day what dinner will be — bluefin tuna from the Genkai Sea, whatever else the morning has offered. A small room, a counter, two tables. One of Japan's Top 100 restaurants, and the meal you'll remember longest.

The City, In Full
market dawn · old town · ramen · last lanterns
09:00·Market
Yanagibashi — Fukuoka's Kitchen
A covered alley barely wider than your shoulders, where forty-seven stalls feed the city's chefs each morning. Glistening tuna belly, slabs of mentaiko, vegetables you've never seen. Saturday is the right day — closed on Sunday. Bring cash, take your time.
📍 Yanagibashi Market ↗
10:30·Old town
Kushida Shrine & the merchant quarter
Fukuoka's spiritual centre, home to the great floats of the Hakata Gion Yamakasa festival — ten metres tall, displayed year-round, impossible not to stop and stare. Next door, a small museum tells the story of Hakata as a merchant city for a thousand years.
📍 Kushida Shrine ↗
12:30·Lunch
Tonkotsu the way it was meant to be — Shin-Shin
Fukuoka invented this dish: pork bones simmered for sixteen hours until the broth turns ivory, thin straight noodles dropped in at the last second, a slice of char siu, a single soft egg. Shin-Shin near Tenjin gets it right every single time.
📍 Shin-Shin ↗
14:00·Afternoon coffee
A second visit to Daimyo
Back to the neighbourhood you already half-know — second cup at Manu, or pull a chair into the sun at CORDUROY, where the windows go floor to ceiling and stay open until three in the morning. Wander the side streets between.

Mentaiko for home

N1 · Airport Transfer
Getting from FUK to the hotel
Taxi from the airport — quickest, easiest, fits all luggage.
A taxi from Fukuoka Airport (International Terminal) to the Ritz-Carlton is roughly ¥2,000 door-to-door, 10–15 minutes. Ask for a large wagon taxi (ワゴン車) at the rank if the queue offers one — comfortable space for two large bags plus a cabin bag.

Awaiting Ritz-Carlton transfer enquiry — they may offer a private car or limousine service from the airport. Compare cost and convenience before deciding.

For departure day — ask the concierge the night before to arrange the taxi or hotel car for 09:30.
N2 · Breakfast
Eating in Daimyo, not at the hotel
Skip the hotel buffet — Daimyo's cafés are better and a fraction of the price.
Hotel Viridis breakfast is ¥6,500 per person — decent, but several reviewers find it overpriced for the quality. Daimyo is at your doorstep: Café Brasileiro, Manu Coffee, and a handful of bakeries all within five minutes. ¥500–1,500 per person, far more memorable, and a better way to begin each day in the city.
N3 · Yanagawa Boat
Pre-book the punt boat for Day 2
Ask the Ritz concierge to book Yanagawa Hissen or Dontaku Suijo Kanko for Fri 29 May around 14:00.
Two main operators run the donko-bune cruises — Yanagawa Hissen (largest fleet) or Dontaku Suijo Kanko. Either is excellent. The ride is 70 minutes, entirely passive, ideal for Gus's recovery — and the afternoon light on the canals is the best of the day.
N4 · Eel Restaurant in Yanagawa
Confirm the seiromushi venue
Ask the concierge to recommend & book Motofuji or Wakamatsuya before Day 2.
"Ohana Restaurant" mentioned in early planning couldn't be independently verified. The two well-established options for seiromushi in Yanagawa are Motofuji and Wakamatsuya. The historic Yanagawa Ohana manor-house also serves it, but requires advance booking — worth asking about if you'd like to try.
